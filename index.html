Biometric Authentication: Facial Recognition    

What is it?

Facial recognition technology is a biometric system that uses artificial intelligence to authenticate a person by analyzing their facial features. The technology captures an image of a user’s face and analyzes key features, such as eye distance, jawline, and nose, and stores them in a secure template or database. When authentication is required, the system converts the template into a cryptographic key and uses it for verification. Once the verification is successful, access is granted to the desired source or application.

Why it matters

Biometric authentication is a widely used security method for mobile devices and protecting users’ private data. Facial recognition offers a convenient and secure way to authenticate users by allowing quick, hands-free access. Its enhanced accuracy and ease of use make it one of the most popular forms of biometric verification today.

Concerns & Security

Facial recognition has expanded far beyond its early applications in the 1990s and is now used in law enforcement, business, and personal technology, raising important questions about consent. Several cases have reached the Supreme Court, contributing to a broader effort to define and protect user data privacy. Additionally, if the technology is not properly developed, it can be less accurate for certain demographic groups, leading to issues of bias and unequal treatment.

application

Real-world uses of facial recognition technology..
Mobile Authentication
Smartphones & Tablets

Facial recognition is widely used on a variety of mobile devices to help unlock the device itself, authenticate logins, and verify payments. For example, Apple’s Face ID used daily, scans users’ faces and compares them to stored data to grant access quickly and securely. This use shows how the technology can enhance convenience while protecting personal data.
Air Travel Security
Airports & Border Control

Airports have adopted facial recognition at security checkpoints. Boarding times dropped by up to 40%, and identity verification averaged 2 seconds.Creating safer and easier ways to travel!
Law Enforcement
Finding Missing People

Facial recognition helps police locate missing persons in large crowds. In India, it helped find over 3,000 missing children in just 4 days by scanning security cameras. This demonstrates how technology can assist critical safety efforts.

  • Consent / Data Privacy

    Social Implications

    Individuals rarely have meaningful ability to opt out, Face data is collected without explicit permission, data is stored and often sold off to the highest bidder. Systems deployed for purpose (security) often overreach and expand to other areas. (Marketing, social media, Law enforcement).

  • Discrimination and Bias

    Risks and harms 

    Higher error rates for women, people of color, elderly, and disabled people. This can reinforce stereotypes through biased training data and denial of access to services, employment opportunties, and public spaces.  

  • Ethics  

    UTILITARIAN / DEONTOLOGICAL 

    Utilitarian:
    PROS: Can reduce crime and improve public safety, improve efficiency in security processes, and more secure access control to private devices.

    CONS: Mass surveillance without proper user consent, storing user data with lack of accountability that erodes data privacy, disproportionate harm to minorities and bias.   

    Deontological:
    PROS: Our government has a duty to protect all citizen, with a right to security and duty to hold violators of data privacy accountable. The vulnerable are entitled to freedom from discrimination and bias with equality under the law. 
     
    CONS: Collecting biometric data treats individuals as an object instead of human with right to privacy. Users can not consent in any meaningful way. Higher error rates for people of color and disabled mean they're not treated with equal respect.

Mobile Authentication

How Face ID stores and uses your data.

Face ID raises several concerns because users worry about how their face data is stored and whether it leaves their devices, though measures are in place to help ease these concerns. For example, Apple addresses this by clarifying that the devices store only an encrypted version of your face in the Secure Enclave, a protected part of the phone that apps and companies can’t access. The data never leaves your device, helping keep your biometric information private and secure. There are also shared concerns about the possible facial spoofing when using Face ID. Apple defends against this with the TrueDepth camera system, which uses advanced technology to ensure its ability to authenticate your true face. ( !

Airport Travel Security

Ensuring Accuracy

Airports use facial recognition to speed up check-ins and boarding, but one major concern is bias: some systems are more likely to misidentify travelers based on skin tone, age, or gender, which could cause delays or unfair treatment. To address this, modern airport systems are trained on diverse datasets that include people of all races, ages, and genders.

Law Enforcement

Balancing Safety and Consent

Facial recognition can help law enforcement identify individuals quickly, but it raises concerns about constant surveillance without consent or knowledge. To address this, some agencies are implementing strict rules about when and how the technology can be used
